Randomness in physical sciences
• What do we mean when we refer to a phenomenon as “random”?
• The outcome of the phenomenon (event, or experiment) cannot be predicted with certainty.
• What are the reasons that we cannot seem to predict the outcomes of the following event or 
experiment?
• Tossing a fair coin and predict the next one is head or tail.
• Weight distribution of the coin? lifting angle? lifting force? air resistance?
• Perform a test to predict the tensile strength of a coupon of steel ASTM A358.
• Measurement system (for example, Instron) precision and accuracy? material 
microstructure difference? material  homogeneity?

10 ± 0.1 𝑚𝑚.
• Caliper or CMM precision and accuracy? location of placing the part using a caliper? manufacturing 
process of the part?

Randomness in other sciences
• In social science, “Hawthorne effect”*:
• People tend to behave differently simply because they are aware of the fact that they are participating 
in an experiment and they are being observed.
• Once a subject knows he/she is being experimented upon, there is an inherent uncertainty associated 
with the outcome of the experiment: Should the observed result be attributed to the treatment or to 
the Hawthorne effect?
• In biomedical science, “Placebo effect”**:
• A “placebo” is a substance having no medical value, but the human subject is told this drug will 
improve his/her state of health.
• The placebo operates psychologically on the individual, whose health then does improve in many 
instances. It is difficult to explain how the psyche induces psychological change. But once the subject 
knows he/she is being experimented upon, a certain inherent randomness in the outcome arises.

Subjective Probability (1/2)
• Suppose in our coin tossing experiment, the coin falls on “heads” on each of 10 
consecutive tosses. If we don’t have prior knowledge of how the coin was fabricated 
and don’t know if the coin has fallen in the past, what are we to believe about the bias 
of the coin? We could consider two possibilities:
• A. The coin is strongly biased towards falling “heads”.
• B. The events of falling “heads” or “tails” are equiprobable (i.e., having equal 
probability), but we just happen to have observed a possible, but unusual run of 
“heads”.
• Should we believe A or B?

Subjective Probability (2/2)
• If we change the 10 consecutive tosses to “all heads out of 20 consecutive tosses”, 
which one do you believe now?
• A. The coin is strongly biased towards falling “heads”
• B. The events of falling “heads” or “tails” are equiprobable (i.e., having equal 
probability), but we just happen to have observed a possible, but unusual run of 
“heads”.
• The accumulating empirical evidence affects our degree of belief about how the coin 
is likely to fall on the next, as yet unobserved, toss. This degree of belief is usually 
called “personal probability” or “subjective probability”.

Ramsey’s Subjective Probability
• The first formal mention of “personal probability” was by Frank Ramsey (1926)*. 
• He argued that probability is not related to a disembodied body of knowledge but is 
related to the knowledge that each individual possesses alone. Thus, personal beliefs 
that are formulated by this individual knowledge govern probabilities, leading to the 
notions of subjective probability and Bayesian probability. 
• Consequently, subjective probabilities can be inferred by observing actions that 
reflect individuals' personal beliefs. Ramsey argued that the degree of probability 
that an individual attaches to a particular outcome can be measured by finding what 
odds the individual would accept when betting on that outcome.

From Classical (Frequentist) Statistics to Bayesian Statistics 
(1/2)
• All the methods and types of analyses described up to now fall in the realm of “classical statistical 
analysis.” 
• We now introduce “Bayesian Methodology” which typically yields more information with smaller sample 
size than classical analysis. The price is “you need to make more assumptions”, and the results could be 
“controversial to the point of being not acceptable by some analysts.” 
• Bayesian methods offer a way of integrating the “past experience” and/or “expert judgement” with the 
testing data towards a goal of reducing sample size and test time. 
• This approach is intuitively attractive when dealing with highly reliable products. 
• On the other hand, some people think this approach might lose the credibility of the final results when 
you could test for nothing but prove something if the “past experience” and/or “expert judgement” are 
quantitatively so convincing that you have already achieved your goal before the testing.

From Classical (Frequentist) Statistics to Bayesian Statistics 
(2/2)
• Classical (frequentist) statistical approaches consider distribution parameters as fixed but unknown 
constants to be estimated using sample data taken randomly from the population of interest. 
• A confidence interval for an unknown parameter is really a frequency statement about the likelihood 
that an interval, calculated after observing one of the conceivably very many possible samples we 
could take from the population, captures the true parameter. It is the interval itself that is random, not 
the parameter value. 
• Bayesian statistical approaches treat these population parameters as uncertain, not fixed, quantities. 
• Before looking at the current data, we use old data/information, or even subjective judgements to 
construct the “prior distribution model” for the population parameters. 
• Through the Bayesian procedures, the estimate of the parameter and confidence intervals (known as 
credibility intervals) are calculated directly from the “posterior distribution model” derived from 
the integration of prior distribution with the current testing data.
• “Credibility intervals” are legitimate probability statements about the unknown parameters.

• Harold Jeffreys' axiomatic system for probability, as presented in his seminal work "Theory of Probability" (1939), was a 
groundbreaking attempt to establish a formal foundation for Bayesian statistics and scientific inference.
• Comparability: Jeffreys proposed that for any two propositions (or statements) regarding uncertain events, their 
probabilities can be compared. This implies that one can always say whether one proposition is more probable than 
another, equally probable, or less probable
• Transitivity: If proposition A is more probable than proposition B, and proposition B is more probable than 
proposition C, then proposition A is more probable than proposition C. This axiom ensures a consistent ordering of 
probabilities.
• Deducibility: If a proposition A can be logically deduced from another proposition B, then the probability of A 
given B is 1 (certainty)
• Product Rule: This is a fundamental axiom in Bayesian probability, and it allows for calculating the joint 
probability of two events.
• Sum Rule: This axiom states that if several propositions are mutually contradictory (mutually exclusive) and 
exhaustive (covering all possibilities), the probability that one of them is true is the sum of the probabilities that 
each separately is true.

Jeffreys’ Axiom System Key Features
--- Page 25 ---
Copyright © Cheng Julius Wang All Rights Reserved
• Probability as a measure of reasonable confidence representing the uncertainty:
• Probability is viewed as a measure of the degree of reasonable confidence one has in the truth of a scientific proposition, rather than solely focusing 
on the limiting frequency of events. 
• Axiomatic derivation of prior distributions:
• Jeffreys proposed an axiomatic way to derive prior distributions. This approach was justified as coherent, compatible with the learning process, and 
capable of incorporating imprecise information.
• Coherent theory of induction:
• He aimed to develop a theory for inductive reasoning, allowing inferences from data and incorporating prior beliefs and uncertainties. He 
recognized the importance of coherence, ensuring that posterior distributions derived from his framework could be used as priors for future 
observations, reflecting the process of learning from data.
• Bayes' Theorem as the core:
• He emphasizes the use of Bayes' theorem to update prior beliefs with new evidence (data) to obtain posterior probabilities.
• Objective Bayes inference:
• His approach tends towards "objective" Bayesian inference, where prior distributions are derived analytically from sampling distributions, and 
examples are treated in a non-informative manner.
• Jeffreys' razor:
• This principle suggests that new parameters in laws should be tested one at a time unless there's a specific reason otherwise, implying a preference 
for simpler models.

Some Core Principles of Bayesian Statistics in 
Reliability Engineering (1/2)
• Core Principle 1: In Bayesian statistics, parameters are treated as “unknown 
quantities.” In classical statistics/probability, these parameters are fixed numbers 
with no uncertainty around them.
• Core Principle 2: For classical statistics, probability is rooted in the limiting relative 
frequency of a certain event in a repeated series of identical trials (for example, 
reliability testing that could be done in a series of similar tests repeatedly, if we have 
sufficient resources to do so). For Bayesian statistics, probability can be considered as 
a “subjective assessment of the state of knowledge” (also called degree of belief) 
about the model parameters of interest given all available evidence.

Some Core Principles of Bayesian Statistics in 
Reliability Engineering (2/2)
• Core Principle 3: The estimate of Bayesian interval (called credible interval) of 
the parameter can be directly interpreted as the probability statement. Most people 
still use the term “Bayesian confidence interval”.
• Core Principle 4: With small sample size and various censoring schemes (multiple  
failure modes/failure mechanisms, various censoring schemes, zero-failure) in most 
industrial settings, Bayesian methods can easily accommodate difficult situations 
given a relevant prior distribution is in place. This often produces narrower bounds 
of interval estimate than that of classical counterpart.
