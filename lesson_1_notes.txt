Introduction – Don’t be fooled by chance!
• Any decision based on limited testing or sampling information will admit the possibility 
of error where an unreliable design may by chance pass the test, whereas a reliable 
design may by chance fail it. 
• The only way to eliminate such sampling error is to test or sample indefinitely. This is 
impossible in almost any business setting.
6
Copyright © Cheng Julius Wang All Rights Reserved
Two Killer Questions… how many to test and for how long?
• To design any reliability test, we need to FIRST a) understand the stressor/load 
distribution (also called customer usage profile, mission profile)…what are they, what 
are the types of failure mechanisms (and failure modes derived from), b) how to apply 
them to test condition, and c) what the acceptance criteria should be.  THEN, the 
following two critical questions are asked:
• How many samples? 
• How long should each sample be tested?
• If sample size is too large, unnecessary costs may be incurred, or resources simply 
cannot afford it in a business setting.
• If sample size is too small, the uncertainty of the reliability estimates will be 
unacceptably high, and the possibility of capturing relevant failures will be low.

Producers vs. Consumers
• The “Producer” produce products.
• The “Consumer” buys products from the Producer.
• The Consumer can be a client (say, an OEM) to the Producer (say, a supplier).
• The product is judged “good” (conforming to a defined specification) or “bad” (non-conformance) by the 
Producer from some defined acceptance test with acceptance criteria.
• Good products are sold to Consumers.
• Bad products are discarded by the Producer.
Copyright © Cheng Julius Wang All Rights Reserved
63
Producer’s Risk and Consumer’s Risk in decision-making
• The Producer may produce GOOD products but fail the acceptance test by chance (sampling error). 
• This is called the Producer’s Risk.
• The Producer may produce BAD products but pass the acceptance test by chance (sampling error). As a 
result, the Consumer may buy a BAD product while believing the product is GOOD. 
• This is called the Consumer’s risk.

What is Hypothesis Testing?
• A hypothesis test is performed by forming two complementary statements about the 
value of a population parameter. For example, 
• Ho: 𝜇 = 3 𝑁𝑚   (called Null Hypothesis) 
• H1: 𝜇 ≠ 3 𝑁𝑚   (called Alternative Hypothesis) 
• We then do the testing to collect the data to see which of the two statements is 
“supported” by the “sample data” (treat this “sample data” as “evidence”) to help 
decision-making.
“Extraordinary claims require extraordinary evidence.”
   - Carl Sagan (1934 – 1996)

Rejecting null hypothesis (Ho) is a strong conclusion!
• Because the results of hypothesis testing are subject to errors, we cannot “prove” or 
“disprove”  a statistical hypothesis. We can, however, design a test procedure to control 
the error probabilities 𝛼 and 𝛽 to suitably small values.
• With the hypothesis testing, we (as experimenter and/or decision-maker) have full 
control of the type I error (𝜶). Therefore, rejecting the null hypothesis (Ho) is usually 
a very strong conclusion that we have substantial evidence to “accept” H1. 
• But the statistical power (𝟏 − 𝜷) depends on what the H1 value is. H1 typically is set as  
H1: 𝜇 ≠ 3, hence it is difficult to control type II error (𝜷).
Copyright © Cheng Julius Wang All Rights Reserved
67
A good practice of setting up the alternative hypothesis (H1) as 
the statement that you want it to happen!
• Since it is difficult to control type II error (𝜷) without knowing what H1 really is before 
the testing, a good practice is to make H1 (alternative hypothesis) represent the 
opportunity of interest or value (that is, the statement that you want it to happen, e.g., 
H1: 𝜇 > 3 𝑁𝑚). And then make Ho (null hypothesis, e.g.,  Ho: 𝜇 = 3 𝑁𝑚) the 
complement of H1. After the testing, if the collected data provide substantial evidence to 
support H1, we can then reject Ho (that is, accept H1) and only commit type I error (𝜶) 
as we decide to control. 
• Note: I chose to use “accept” or “reject” a hypothesis. Some textbooks follow strict guidelines of using “can’t 
reject” a hypothesis, and this sometimes causes confusion and is awkward to apply. I have chosen to violate the 
rule for convenience reason.
• Remember this point: Hypothesis testing is more about the alternative hypothesis (H1) 
than the null hypothesis (Ho) in general practices.
