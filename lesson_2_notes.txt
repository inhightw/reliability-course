• Reliability Demonstration Test (RDT) is concerned with a decision (pass or fail) and the amount of data to 
collect. It is to decide between two hypotheses (equipment is good vs. equipment is “not so good”), which 
must be numerically defined. In the testing hypothesis setup, the “equipment is “not so good”” is set up as 
the minimum acceptable reliability or MTBF (or the maximum failure rate) as H1.
• The test carries a probabilistic guarantee (the statistical power) that certain risks will not be exceeded. 
Typically, a contract MTBF (or failure rate, or a reliability% target at a specific time/cycle) is specified.
• To reduce/control the risk of making a wrong decision from testing:
• Measurement system must be precise and accurate: Gage R&R for functional/performance measures.
• Statistical method is used to determine the following while controlling Type I, Type II, or both risks:
• How many to test
• How long to test

• Two general statistical inference methods in determining the required sample size:
• The Estimation Approach
• The goal is to determine the effect of sample size on the precision of the estimated reliability

• Sample size is determined based on the desired confidence interval width.
• The Risk Control Approach
• The goal is to control the Type I and/or Type II errors.
• Type I error (producer’s risk): Reject a product that meets the reliability requirement
• Type II error (consumer’s risk): Accept a product that does not meet the requirement
